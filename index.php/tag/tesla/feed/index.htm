What is Financial Performance

The overall financial performance incorporates all revenue, costs, and expenses for a period. The three main financial statements include an income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow statement. Quarterly reports are also issued to give investors an in-depth look at how a company is doing and what its future outlook may be. A good way to measure overall financial performance is through profitability ratios such as return on equity (ROE) or return on assets (ROA). These ratios provide insight into how well a company manages its resources and whether it’s able to generate enough profit from those resources.

What are costs, Income and Important Ratios

Some important ratios that entrepreneurs should track are gross margin, operating margin, return on invested capital (ROIC), and cash flow. Gross profit is an indication of how much money a company makes from selling goods and services to customers. Net profit is what’s left over after all expenses have been paid—or, in other words, net income. Gross Margin

The gross margin is one of, if not THE most important numbers for any publicly-traded company. Essentially, it’s a snapshot of how much money is left over from revenue after all expenses are paid out. It helps investors understand how healthy a company’s business really is in comparison to its competitors and gives insight into how sustainable a company’s earnings may be in relation to its stock price.

EBIT (Earnings Before Interest & Tax)

When a company reports earnings per share (EPS), it also reports EBIT or earnings before interest and tax. Like EPS, EBIT is reported on a per-share basis. However, it does not take into account interest expenses and taxes, which EPS does. Often times these two line items are added back to give investors a better picture of how much money an organization actually earned in a given year. In other words, EBIT is essentially an adjusted EPS number. For example, if a company has $100 million in revenue and $50 million in net income but $10 million in interest expense and $5 million in income tax, its adjusted net income would be calculated as follows: ($100M – $10M – $5M) / 10M = 10% operating margin.

Net Profit Margin Section: Equity Profit Margin

If you look at a company’s annual income statement, you can usually find a line called net income. This line item is equal to earnings before interest and taxes (EBIT) because net income – EBIT = profits. When calculating profit margin, we subtract out net interest expense from both sides of that equation to get: earnings before tax (EBT) – debit expenses = profit margin. Next, we divide that number by sales to arrive at our final margin figure.

Return on Assets (ROA)

The Return on Assets (ROA) formula is a profitability ratio that measures a company’s profitability relative to its total assets. ROA helps investors in determining how efficient management is at using its assets to generate earnings. To calculate return on assets, subtract total liabilities from a company’s net income and divide by total assets. A higher number means that a company is earning more profit for each dollar of assets. Investors can use ROA to compare companies within an industry or across different industries. A high return on assets indicates that a company is able to earn profits despite having few tangible assets or large amounts of intangible assets.

Return on Equity (ROE) Section: Quick Ratio/Acid Test Ratio

Return on Equity (ROE) is a measure that shows how much profit a company makes in comparison to shareholder’s equity. To calculate ROE, you divide income available to common stockholders by book value. The quick ratio or acid test ratio is a liquidity ratio used to assess whether a business has enough cash or easily convertible assets to meet its short-term obligations. The formula for calculating it is: Quick Ratio = (Cash + Accounts Receivable + Marketable Securities)/Current Liabilities.
