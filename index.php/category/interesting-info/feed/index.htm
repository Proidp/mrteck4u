What makes English the world’s most powerful language? Is it the way it sounds? The way it’s written? The sheer number of people who speak it? Read on to find out!

Why did the world start speaking English?

When discussing how to learn English in a simple, effective manner, I often hear people say something like: I’m never going to need it. I live in Brazil; Spanish is enough for me. While you might think that speaking Spanish will allow you to get by just fine, I’d argue that there are a couple of reasons why every single person should be learning English—even if they don’t plan on leaving their home country. The world has become an increasingly interconnected place, and with increased globalization comes increased opportunities. Even if you don’t see yourself ever traveling outside your home country or region, learning English opens up new career paths that would otherwise be closed off to non-English speakers. It also makes doing business easier, as many companies have a worldwide presence and knowing English allows you to work with clients from all over the world.

What Makes a Language Universal?

In a world of seven billion people, hundreds of spoken languages, and thousands of dead ones, English is becoming an increasingly important unifier. That may sound obvious—English is one of six official languages at United Nations headquarters in New York City—but it belies some seriously impressive numbers. According to SIL International, an estimated 400 million people speak some form of English as a second language; that’s about 1 in 10 people on Earth. By comparison, China only has an estimated 100 million native speakers of Mandarin Chinese. While that may be great for creating global understanding, it comes with its own problems; deciding how to speak or write proper English can often lead to arguments both within and across countries.

Native Speakers Today

How Did It Happen? There are around a billion people who speak English as their first language and roughly 3 billion people who speak it as a second language. These figures make English one of our planet’s most widely-spoken languages; but how did it become so dominant? The history of an international language is fascinating in its own right, but also offers valuable insights into how we communicate today. In particular, what can we learn about globalization from English’s meteoric rise to prominence? And how will English change over time?
English is no longer just a Western European language – these days, you’ll find speakers all over Asia, Africa and South America. This geographic spread isn’t surprising when you consider that 500 years ago, there were only 10 million native speakers of English worldwide! However, by 1750 there were 50 million native speakers (and another 50 million non-native ones), which means that by 1850 – just 100 years later – there were 100 million native speakers worldwide.

How Many People Speak English as their First Language?

As a result of colonization, there are hundreds of millions of people around the world who speak English as their first language. These nations include countries in North America and across Europe, Australia, and South Africa. There are also many former colonies that still use English to varying degrees, including India, Nigeria, Liberia, Malaysia and Pakistan. In total there are more than a billion people around the world who speak English as their native language.

What are Some Confusing Points in the History of English?

England was originally invaded by Germanic tribes who spoke Germanic languages. These were later replaced with Scandinavian invaders, most of whom also spoke Germanic languages, although some were (vaguely) related to Old Norse. Finally, Latin-speaking Normans from France conquered England in 1066 and ruled it for more than 300 years, bringing French with them but also learning English.

We’d all like to think we know what words mean, but some are up for debate. Take Spanish. Is it a language? A dialect? An accent? Is it Spanish with an American twang or something else entirely? So…How Can You Learn To Speak It Like a Native Speaker?

For those of you who are not native speakers, how can you learn to speak it like a native speaker? The best way is to immerse yourself in an English-speaking environment. This will force you to listen and speak only in English. Every time you speak or interact with another person, you’ll be exposed to grammar rules and new vocabulary. You’ll also start picking up on slang words that native speakers use all of the time. It won’t happen overnight, but if you put in enough hours and practice every day, you should be able to sound just as good as a native speaker within a few months’ time.

It’s hard to believe that 15 years ago, Elon Musk didn’t even have one million dollars to his name. But today, the South African-born serial entrepreneur has a net worth of over 20 billion dollars and the founder of SpaceX, Tesla Motors, and Neuralink. How did he do it? You might be surprised to learn what Musk’s biggest strengths are as an entrepreneur and business leader.

Tesla

The idea for Tesla started with a hot tub. While vacationing in Ontario, Canada, at his friend’s cottage, Musk saw that all of his friend’s electricity was coming from a hydroelectric plant built by Nikola Tesla in 1895—the same year as Edison opened his first power plant. Musk wondered why no one had thought of connecting solar panels to batteries before and began researching how he could do it himself. He later told 60 Minutes: I came up with a design for a solar thermal energy system that I thought would be low cost and simple and easy to install…and then I worked on figuring out what would be a good market for it. And I thought, well, we need something where there’s lots of sunlight and not much air pollution.

SpaceX

The goal of SpaceX is both ambitious and grounded in reality. If we can send a vehicle into space and return it safely to Earth, then commercial space travel—and ultimately colonization of other planets—becomes possible. And that means taking humanity off planet. It’s a huge ambition but with proper planning and execution, it’s not as crazy as it sounds.

SolarCity

When Elon Musk started SolarCity in 2006, he never expected it would become a multi-billion dollar company. Back then, solar energy was just becoming popular, and few people were aware of its benefits or how much it would cost. To figure out how much investment would be needed to launch a massive-scale project, he called his cousin Lyndon Rive (one of SolarCity’s co-founders) and asked him how much money they could raise if they sold their shares at $10 each. The answer? Approximately $80 million—but that’s only if everyone invested.

Hyperloop

In 2012, a white paper written by Musk became popular. In it, he described a new form of high-speed transportation that would travel faster than supersonic jets. He called it the fifth mode of transportation — after planes, trains, cars and boats — that would carry passengers in pressurized tubes at near-supersonic speeds. Unlike trains or cars, Hyperloop capsules traveling between distant cities wouldn’t need to stop for hours at a time while they load and unload passengers and cargo. The whole idea came together under an umbrella concept: Hyperloop.

OpenAI

The nonprofit artificial intelligence research company OpenAI, launched by Tesla and SpaceX founder Elon Musk, is hiring. The San Francisco-based team focuses on developing AI that benefits humanity as a whole, instead of helping a specific business or creating weapons. According to its site, Our goal is to advance digital intelligence in the way that is most likely to benefit humanity as a whole, unconstrained by a need to generate a financial return. While few companies have made major strides in improving AIs over time (Google’s DeepMind being one), OpenAI has some big backers (and brains) behind it.

Neuralink

Why the risks are worth it
Entrepreneurs often take risks when they start a business. There’s risk in starting a company, whether it’s driven by passion or profit, and for some of us, it may seem like there’s an endless supply of ways our businesses could fail. When these fears loom large, it can be difficult to know if we should push forward with our idea or cut our losses. But what many entrepreneurs forget is that failure is inevitable—there are a lot of things that could go wrong. That said, even those who fail don’t give up on their entrepreneurial dreams: According to Small Business Trends research, 70 percent of people who started businesses felt more confident in their decision after they had failed.


Elon Musk has long been fascinated with artificial intelligence and how it might change humanity. He says that AI will not just be a mix of human intelligence and machine intelligence, but it will ultimately take on a life of its own. To that end, he launched Neuralink, which aims to connect your brain directly to digital devices like computers. His belief is that in order for humans and AI to work together we must merge with machines — if we don’t do so willingly, he believes our own evolution as a species could force us into it against our will. One way or another, he wants humans and machines to become one.

The financial services sector has more competition

Musk could have gotten his degree in physics, joined a big company, and lived happily ever after. But he didn’t. He took a chance on PayPal because he thought there was more opportunity in financial services than rocketry. That might seem obvious now, but back then it was a much riskier idea — so risky that eBay beat him out by acquiring PayPal for $1.5 billion while he was still at school.

Raising Capital is becoming more difficult

The good news is that raising capital is getting easier. The bad news? Raising capital is becoming more difficult. A decade ago, it took startups an average of 24 months to raise their first significant round of venture funding. Today, it takes more than three years. While total funding has grown by 30% during that time, rounds have shrunk by 20%. This means you will likely have to work harder and smarter than ever before if you want your business startup business gets funded.

What he would have done differently

It’s hard to say with certainty what Musk would have done differently in his younger days, but there’s at least one thing he has mentioned since becoming a billionaire: I was really sad when I realized I wouldn’t become a superhero. When I was a kid, superheroes were my idols. It would be great if we could create some kind of fast transportation system—you know, go anywhere on Earth in under an hour. That would be fantastic. The only way to do that would be to have an orbital vehicle…and re-enter from orbit. These things sound far out, but they’re really not that crazy anymore…the technology is more or less there.

Introduction

On March 8, 2014, Malaysia Airlines Flight 370 departed from Kuala Lumpur International Airport in Malaysia en route to Beijing. When it didn’t land as scheduled in Beijing four hours later, authorities around Asia and then the world launched a search operation that remains ongoing two years later.
